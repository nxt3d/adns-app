---
title: Naming conventions
description: Why an aDNS name reads name.sub, and how the public form maps to storage.
---

An aDNS name has the public form:

```text
name.sub.sub
```

For example: `normies.yoel`.

## The label you own comes first

In DNS and ENS, the most specific part is on the left and the root is on the right
(`alice.eth`, `mail.google.com`). aDNS inverts this for the *public* form so the label
**you register leads**:

- `normies.yoel` — `yoel` is registered under the `normies` namespace.
- `pudgypenguins.charlie` — `charlie` is registered under `pudgypenguins`.

The leftmost segment is the collection or parent namespace; the segment after it is your
second-level name. You read it outward: *the* `yoel` *of* `normies`.

## Backwards by default

Internally, aDNS stores names in the conventional out-to-in order — the same order ENS
resolvers expect. The public `normies.yoel` corresponds to the stored name with `yoel`
as the second-level label beneath the `normies` parent.

<Callout type="info">
  Rule of thumb: the **aDNS form** (`normies.yoel`) is what you read, share, and type into
  the registration widget. The **stored form** is what the contracts and resolvers see. The
  app handles the conversion for you.
</Callout>

## Levels

- **Apex** — reserved for the protocol.
- **First level** — collection slugs (e.g. `normies`, `pudgypenguins`). This is the parent
  namespace your name lives under.
- **Second level** — the name you register and own (e.g. `yoel`, `charlie`).

Names are issued as real labels. There are no numeric ids to remember: the label you
register is the label everyone sees.
